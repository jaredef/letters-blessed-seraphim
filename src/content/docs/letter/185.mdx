September 12/25, 1975,

Celebration of the Nativity of the Virgin Mary.

Dear Father Georgiy,

Blessings!

In this letter, at your request, we are sending brief information about Western saints. None of them, it seems, are in the Orthodox calendar, but except for one (St. Callistus, who should be studied more thoroughly), they are undoubtedly authentic Orthodox saints of the West.

We are gradually accumulating information about Western saints and coming to some conclusions about how to distinguish true Orthodox saints of the West (which, of course, includes the majority of Western saints before the schism) from the few that are doubtful or even definitely not Orthodox for one reason or another. Since we simply cannot trust any Western sources of recent centuries, and practically most Western sources starting from the 11th century (when knightly romances and legends began to appear), we must return as close as possible to the original sources and, when possible, connect them with reliable Orthodox sources both from the East and the West. I have tried to briefly do this in the attached message, and we will also publish an article on this topic in the 1976 calendar (for which your order is recorded), if God permits. Please pray for us so that we can continue this work in fulfillment of the mandate given to us by Archbishop Ioann.

Regarding the veneration of saints who are not in Orthodox calendars, there have been various approaches to this throughout different periods of Orthodox history and in different places, and there is no rule regulating this. Waiting for the official "canonization" of all these saints would be fruitless and hopeless (and very sad for us who love them!), and in fact, it has never been done - usually lists of local saints or saints of other Orthodox Churches are added to the Orthodox calendar much less formally. But it is also obvious that since there are still some questions concerning at least a few Western saints before the schism, we should not be too free in venerating just anyone we want. In your case, so that you are not unsure that you are doing something wrong or introducing some innovation (and almost as important - to avoid unfounded rumors that you are doing so), we would advise you to present this list with an explanatory letter to your bishop (it seems, Archbishop Nikon?) asking him to give permission to venerate these saints in your church (i.e., to have their relics, icons, celebrate them, and perform services to them). You can present him with our observations, or if you prefer, we can write him a letter in Russian (we know him well), providing our arguments why these saints can be considered Orthodox. (But Callistus should be studied more thoroughly before submitting his name with the rest.)

Is there any possibility for us to obtain relics of Western saints? We would especially like to have a particle of the relics of St. Scholastica, and we would greatly appreciate it if we could obtain relics of St. Martin of Tours or St. Gregory of Tours, or other Gallic saints (or any Western saints at all!).

We are glad that you have a new church building. May Christ our Lord grant prosperity to your parish in true Orthodoxy! But be prepared for the coming severe trials and do not let them knock you off your feet!

Please, pray for us, sinners!

With love in Christ,

Monk Seraphim.

Brotherhood of St. Herman of Alaska,

Platina, California,

Father Seraphim.

NOTES ON SEVEN ORTHODOX SAINTS OF THE WEST, WHOSE NAMES ARE NOT IN THE ORTHODOX CALENDAR.

[1.] Saint Clement, Pope of Rome, April 26.

Evidence. Ancient martyrologies call him a "martyr," which is sufficient, as there is no evidence that he was unorthodox to establish his veneration.

Note. The Roman martyrology considers him the third Pope (April 26), and Anacletus the fifth Pope (July 13), but Eusebius thought they were the same person, and modern scholars, it seems, lean towards this opinion. In fact, almost nothing is known about either of them, except that they were martyrs.

[2.] Saint Callistus, Pope of Rome, p. 218-222. October 14 (apparently the date of his burial, and martyrdom, according to Butler, October 12).

Evidence. Ancient martyrologies call him a martyr.

Difficulties. Saint Hippolytus of Rome strongly criticized him (1) for being too lenient in matters of repentance (which in itself does not make him wrong), (2) for preaching the Patripassian heresy of Noetus (a serious accusation).

Conclusion. He should be studied more thoroughly, for example, through the ecclesiastical histories of Socrates and Sozomen (they exist in English in the Erdman series, but we do not have them) and Theodore (possibly available in English).

[3.] Saint Julius, Pope of Rome, +352, April 12.

Evidence. Well known in Church history as a defender of Saint Athanasius the Great against the Arians, he even sent Saint Athanasius back to Alexandria with documents in his favor. In the Russian synodal life of Saint Athanasius, he is referred to as "Saint Julius." There seems to be no doubt about his Orthodoxy and sanctity.

[4.] Saint Scholastica, +543, February 10.

Evidence. In the "Dialogues" of Saint Gregory the Great, book II (Life of Saint Benedict), chapters 33-34, there is an account of miracles when she detained her brother's departure, causing a storm through prayer, and Saint Benedict himself saw her ascending to Heaven after her death. This is almost all that is known about her, but the testimony of these two Orthodox saints leaves no doubt about her sanctity.

[5.] Saint Eugene, Bishop of Carthage, 505, July 13.

Evidence. Well known in church history as a defender of Orthodoxy against Arianism, he suffered and was persecuted under Arian Vandals. Gennadius attributes sermons against Arians to him, and Saint Gregory of Tours ("History of the Franks") quotes a letter from his exile to his flock. There is no doubt about his Orthodoxy and sanctity.

[6.] Saint Ursula, 3rd century, October 21.

Evidence. A large church was built over her relics (and those of other virgin martyrs) in Cologne, much popular devotion in the 7th century and possibly earlier, inscription from the 4th century about two martyrs (without names).

Difficulties. Her life was written late and includes many inventions and legends, and her name does not appear in texts until the 9th century (although veneration of the Cologne martyrs began much earlier).

Conclusion. It is better not to trust her life composed from Catholic sources, but there is reason to doubt that she was a virgin martyr of the ancient period of the Church, about whom, perhaps, nothing more can be known.

[7.] Saint Eligius, Bishop of Noyon, 659, December 1.

Evidence. An extensive life written by his friend, Saint Ouen, Bishop of Rouen, 13 years after his death (according to other sources, 50 years later). Many miracles, popular veneration. No evidence of unorthodoxy.
